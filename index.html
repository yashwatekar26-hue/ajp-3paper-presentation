Paper 1 · Review

AJP Technology: The Foundation

Wilkinson et al. (2019) · Int J Adv Manuf Technol

📄 Read the Abstract

"Aerosol Jet Printing (AJP) is an emerging contactless direct write approach... Freeform deposition, coupled with a relatively large stand-off distance, enables increased geometric complexity. Wide material compatibility, high resolution and independence of orientation provide novelty... This overview summarises the principles, reviews applications and discusses hurdles to industry adoption."

⚙️ How It Works

Atomization & Deposition

Inks are turned into a mist (aerosol) using either ultrasonic waves (for low-viscosity inks) or pneumatic gas (for high-viscosity inks). The mist is then focused by a sheath gas and deposited with a 1-5 mm stand-off, allowing printing on curved surfaces.

📦 Materials

From Silver to DNA

The key strength is versatility. Researchers have printed silver nanoparticles for electronics, ceramics for capacitors, and even proteins and DNA for biosensors—all using the same machine.

⚠️ The Bottleneck

Overspray & Ink Challenges

Two main issues remain: Overspray (tiny satellite droplets that ruin line edges) and complex ink formulation. Unlike inkjet printing, there is no universal formula to predict if an ink will work.

📊 What they achieved: The review shows that AJP can produce ~10 µm fine features. However, it also highlights a major gap: the process is highly empirical, and achieving consistent quality requires extensive trial-and-error.

Paper 2 · Innovation

Solving the 60-Year Dispersion Problem

Hwang et al. (2024) · Nano Energy

📄 Read the Abstract

"Polymer-based nanocomposites emerged in the 1960s... however, achieving uniform nanomaterial dispersion has impeded progress. Here, we present a polymer-based nanocomposite with highly dispersed nanomaterials, achieved through aerosol-jet-based multi-material three-dimensional (AM3D) printing. Numerical simulations facilitate the avoidance of interfacial compatibility issues... As a result, the 3D structured nanocomposite exhibits a uniform dielectric constant and low dielectric loss."

❌ Traditional Pre-Mixing

Nanoparticles clump together (agglomerate), leading to weak spots and electrical leaks.

✅ AM3D (Separate Atomization)

Perfectly dispersed nanoparticles. Uniform properties across the entire film.

💡 The "Graffiti" Trick

Don't Pre-Mix!

Inspired by graffiti art, the researchers atomize the polymer and the nanoparticles separately. They only combine in the printhead at the very last moment. This completely prevents the agglomeration that happens when you mix them in a jar.

🔬 The Result

16× Better Performance

By eliminating agglomeration, the material's dielectric constant increased dramatically (up to 16 times higher in multi-layer structures). The electrical properties were uniform, and the material performed excellently in energy-harvesting devices.

Paper 3 · Breakthrough

Electric Field Guided 3D Nanoprinting

Jung et al. (2019) · Science (submitted/preprint style)

📄 Read the Abstract

"We propose a novel 3D nano-printing concept... We apply a floating dielectric mask to focus charged aerosols through a convergent electrostatic field... Interestingly, we found two different regimes (3D growth mode and 3D writing mode) exist depending on the translation velocity of the stage."

Animation: Particles focus through the mask hole onto the growing tip.

⚡ The "Electric Funnel"

Electrostatic Focusing

Instead of a physical nozzle, they use electric fields. Ions accumulate on a mask, creating a funnel that guides charged nanoparticles with extreme precision. This removes the physical constraints of a nozzle.

🌀 Two Modes of Printing

Grow or Write

Growth Mode: Move the stage slowly, and particles build tall 3D pillars or spirals. Writing Mode: Move fast, and the particles "brush" across the substrate, allowing you to draw letters or patterns.

📐 Key Concept: "Persistence Length"
The team defined a concept called "persistence length" to predict how the electric field line bends. This allowed them to intentionally build complex 3D shapes like helices, zig-zags, and even downward-growing structures—something never done before at this scale.

🏗️ 3D Structures

Helices & Zig-zags

Fabricated uniform helices, zig-zags, and stair-like structures by simply programming the stage movement.

✍️ Writing Mode

Letters "3D"

Demonstrated writing of the letters "3" and "D" using different materials (Palladium and Copper) in the same process.

📏 The Big Win

< 100 nm Resolution

This technique achieves sub-100 nm resolution—100 times smaller than conventional AJP (10 µm). It brings true 3D nanostructures into reality.

🔗 The Missing Link

Paper 3 makes tiny, complex 3D shapes but only with one metal at a time.
Paper 2 makes perfect multi-material blends but can't make complex 3D nano-shapes.
Paper 1 shows us the starting point. We are missing a technique that combines multi-material mixing + 3D nano-resolution.

Research Question

The Next Logical Step

"How can we integrate the electric-field 3D nanoprinting from Paper 3
with the separate-atomization multi-material trick from Paper 2,
to achieve multi-material, true 3D nanostructures in a single process?"

🎯 Why Ask This?

  • ✓ P3 can print nano-scale 3D shapes, but only with one material.
  • ✓ P2 can print multi-materials perfectly, but only flat or thick layers.
  • ✓ Combining them is the holy grail: "Multi-material 3D Nano-printing".

🔬 Where This Could Go

  • ✓ 3D Nano-electronics: Complex circuits at the molecular scale.
  • ✓ Smart Sensors: Combining different materials for multi-sensing in one tiny 3D structure.
  • ✓ Metamaterials: 3D nanostructures with properties that don't exist in nature.
