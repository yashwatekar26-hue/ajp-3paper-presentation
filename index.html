Paper 1 · Review

AJP Technology: The Foundation

Wilkinson et al. (2019) · A review of aerosol jet printing for micro-manufacturing

📄 Read the Abstract

"Aerosol Jet Printing (AJP) is an emerging contactless direct write approach aimed at the production of fine features on a wide range of substrates. Originally developed for the manufacture of electronic circuitry, the technology has been explored for a range of applications, including active and passive electronic components, actuators, sensors, as well as a variety of selective chemical and biological responses. Freeform deposition, coupled with a relatively large stand-off distance, is enabling researchers to produce devices with increased geometric complexity. Wide material compatibility, high resolution and independence of orientation have provided novelty... This overview will summarise the underlying principles, review applications and discuss hurdles to widespread industry adoption."

⚙️ Mechanism

Two Atomization Methods

Ultrasonic (1-10 cP) for low viscosity. Pneumatic (1-1000 cP) for high viscosity. Droplets are focused via a sheath gas and deposited with a 1-5 mm stand-off.

📦 Materials

Extremely Wide Compatibility

Silver nanoparticle inks, ceramics (Barium Titanate), polymers (SU-8), and even biological matter like proteins and DNA strands.

⚠️ Challenges

Overspray & Ink Formulation

Overspray (satellite droplets) ruins edge definition. Ink formulation requires extensive empirical work, lacking a universal "Z-number" like inkjet printing.

📊 Key Data: Finest reported conductive lines: 10 µm (Cai et al.). Resistances roughly double that of bulk silver due to porosity. Bending endurance is limited to < 10,000 cycles for flexible applications.

Paper 2 · Innovation

Solving the 60-Year Dispersion Problem

Hwang et al. (2024) · Highly dispersed nanomaterials in polymer matrix via AM3D printing

📄 Read the Abstract

"Polymer-based nanocomposites emerged in the 1960s... however, over the past six decades, the challenges of achieving uniform nanomaterial dispersion and the resulting non-uniform properties have impeded further progress. Here, we present a polymer-based nanocomposite with highly dispersed nanomaterials, achieved through aerosol-jet-based multi-material three-dimensional (AM3D) printing. This method allows precise programming of the nanocomposite's composition, structure, and dispersity. Numerical simulations facilitate the avoidance of interfacial compatibility issues... As a result, the 3D structured nanocomposite exhibits a uniform dielectric constant and low dielectric loss across the entire printed area."

❌ Pre-Mixing (Agglomeration)

Clusters cause stress concentration & electrical leakage.

✅ AM3D (Separate Atomization)

Perfect dispersion. Uniform mechanical & electrical properties.

Animation: Pre-mixing causes agglomeration (pink clusters) vs. AM3D provides perfect dispersion (blue dots).

💡 Core Idea

Graffiti Art Inspiration

Just as graffiti artists spray different colors separately onto a wall, AM3D atomizes polymer solution and nanomaterial suspension independently. They combine only at the printhead → zero pre-mixing agglomeration.

🔬 Key Results

16x Dielectric Enhancement

SEM shows no agglomeration. Dielectric constant (ε′) is highly uniform across the film. Multi-layer structures achieved 16x higher ε′ with low loss tangent. Demonstrated in TENG devices for energy harvesting.

📊 Key Data: Single-layer ε′ = 28 (vs 14 for pure PVDF-TrFE). Multi-layer sandwiched structure achieved 16x improvement. Open-circuit voltage (Voc) in TENG increased from 80V to ~90V.

Paper 3 · Breakthrough

Electric Field Guided 3D Nanoprinting

Jung et al. (2019) · Three-dimensional nanoprinting via charged aerosol focusing

📄 Read the Abstract

"Three-dimensional (3D) printing has attracted great attention... ensuring nanoscale resolution with high degree of freedom... Herein, we propose a novel 3D nano-printing concept... We apply a floating dielectric mask concept combined with 3D translation of piezoelectric nanostage to focus charged aerosols through convergent electrostatic field through apertures in the floating mask... Interestingly, we found two different regimes (3D growth mode and 3D writing mode) exist depending on the translation velocity of the stage."

Animation: Charged particles focus through the mask hole onto the growing pillar tip.

⚡ Core Principle

Electrostatic Focusing Lens

Positive ions accumulate on the floating mask, creating a "cushion" of charge. This distorts the electric field, forming narrow funnels above the holes that guide charged nanoparticles onto the substrate.

🌀 Two Modes

Growth vs. Writing

Growth Mode (slow stage): Nanoparticles accumulate at the tip, building 3D structures (pillars, helices). Writing Mode (fast stage): Particles deposit directly on the substrate, acting like a "brush".

📐 Theoretical Innovation: Introduces the "Persistence Length" (Lp) to describe how the electric field line acts as a flexible drawing tool. "R = Lp * sin(θ/2)". With Lp ≈ 1 µm, they predicted and built downward-growing structures and helices with < 100 nm resolution.

🏗️ 3D Structures

Helices & Zig-zags

Fabricated uniform helices with 1 µm radius, pitch angle ~45°, and complex zig-zag and stair structures by programming the stage movement.

✍️ Writing Mode

Letters "3D"

Demonstrated writing of letters "3" and "D" using different materials (Pd and Cu), confirmed by EDS mapping.

📏 Resolution

< 100 nm

True nanoscale 3D printing. Outperforms conventional AJP (10 µm) by two orders of magnitude.

Research Question

The Next Logical Step

"How can we integrate the electrostatic-guided 3D nanoprinting approach from Paper 3
with the separate-atomization multi-material strategy from Paper 2,
to achieve multi-material, true 3D nanostructures with sub-100 nm resolution in a single hybrid process?"

🎯 Why This Question

  • ✓ P3 proves we can print sub-100 nm 3D structures, but only with single metals.
  • ✓ P2 proves we can perfectly mix polymers + nanoparticles, but only at the micro-scale.
  • ✓ Merging them creates "Multi-material 3D Nano-printing" – a new manufacturing paradigm.

🔬 Potential Impact

  • ✓ 3D Nano-electronics: Build complex, multi-material interconnects at the nanoscale.
  • ✓ Heterogeneous Sensors: Integrate different functional materials (e.g., dielectric + conductor) in one 3D nano-architecture.
  • ✓ Meta-materials: Design 3D nano-structures with custom optical/mechanical properties impossible with single materials.
