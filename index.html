Paper 1 · Review

AJP Technology: The Foundation

Wilkinson et al. (2019) · Int J Adv Manuf Technol

📄 Read the Abstract

"Aerosol Jet Printing (AJP) is an emerging contactless direct write approach aimed at the production of fine features on a wide range of substrates. Originally developed for the manufacture of electronic circuitry... Freeform deposition, coupled with a relatively large stand-off distance, is enabling researchers to produce devices with increased geometric complexity. Wide material compatibility, high resolution and independence of orientation have provided novelty... This overview will summarise the underlying principles, review applications and discuss hurdles to widespread industry adoption."

🎯Focusing

🧪Substrate
⚙️ Atomization

Viscosity Ranges

Ultrasonic: 1–10 cP (monodisperse). Pneumatic: 1–1000 cP (polydisperse, requires virtual impactor). Stand-off: 1–5 mm enables conformal printing on 3D surfaces (e.g., golf balls).

📦 Materials

Silver Inks to DNA

Ag nanoparticle inks (resistance ~2× bulk silver), ceramics (BaTiO₃), SU-8 photoresist, and biological matter (proteins, DNA). Material compatibility is a key novelty.

⚠️ Key Challenges

Overspray & Formulation

Overspray from satellite droplets degrades line quality. No universal "Z-number" exists for ink formulation—unlike inkjet printing—leading to extensive empirical optimisation.

📊 Specific Data: Finest reported conductive lines: 10 µm (Cai et al.). Bending endurance is limited to < 10,000 cycles for flexible substrates. Photopolymer optical waveguides achieved attenuation of 0.7 dB/cm after surface conditioning.

Paper 2 · Innovation

Solving the 60-Year Dispersion Problem

Hwang et al. (2024) · Nano Energy

📄 Read the Abstract

"Polymer-based nanocomposites emerged in the 1960s... however, the challenges of achieving uniform nanomaterial dispersion have impeded further progress. Here, we present a polymer-based nanocomposite with highly dispersed nanomaterials, achieved through aerosol-jet-based multi-material three-dimensional (AM3D) printing. Numerical simulations (Euler-Lagrangian) facilitate the avoidance of interfacial compatibility issues... As a result, the 3D structured nanocomposite exhibits a uniform dielectric constant and low dielectric loss across the entire printed area."

❌ Pre-Mixing (Agglomeration)

Clusters cause stress concentration & electrical leakage.

✅ AM3D (Separate Atomization)

Isotropic printing. Perfect dispersion & uniform properties.

💡 Core Strategy

Graffiti Art Inspiration

Atomizes PI/PVDF-TrFE polymer and Ag nanoparticles separately (Pneumatic + Ultrasonic). They combine only at the 114° junction printhead. No pre-mixing = zero agglomeration.

🔬 Key Results

16× Dielectric Enhancement

Single-layer ε′ = 28 vs 14 (pure PVDF-TrFE) at 1 kHz. Multi-layer sandwich structure yields 16× improvement with low loss tangent (0.02). TENG Voc increased from 80V to ~90V.

📊 Process Parameters: Low UA flow rates (5–25 sccm) yield isotropic mixing. ~200 nm thickness per layer. Numerical simulation (Euler-Lagrangian) identified 114° junction angle as optimal to minimize stagnation zones and anisotropic clustering.

Paper 3 · Breakthrough

Electric Field Guided 3D Nanoprinting

Jung et al. (2019) · Science (submitted/preprint style)

📄 Read the Abstract

"We propose a novel 3D nano-printing concept... We apply a floating dielectric mask concept combined with 3D translation of piezoelectric nanostage to focus charged aerosols through convergent electrostatic field through apertures in the floating mask... Interestingly, we found two different regimes (3D growth mode and 3D writing mode) exist depending on the translation velocity of the stage."

Animation: Particles focus through the 4 µm mask hole onto the growing tip.

⚡ Mechanism

Electrostatic Focusing Lens

Positive ions accumulate on the SiNₓ floating mask (4 µm holes). This distorts the electric field, creating narrow funnels that guide charged Pd/Cu nanoparticles (~50 nm size) onto the substrate.

🌀 Two Modes

Growth vs. Writing

Growth: Slow stage (< 50 µm/h) → tip accumulation (pillars, helices). Writing: Fast stage (25 nm/s) → particles deposit directly as a "brush" (letters "3D").

📐 Theoretical Innovation: Introduced "Persistence Length" (Lp ≈ 1.12 µm). Equation: R = Lp · sin(θ/2). This allowed prediction of downward-growing structures and steady-state helices. Helix radius = 1 µm, pitch angle ≈ 41° with rotation speed ω ≈ 4π h⁻¹.

🏗️ 3D Structures

Helices & Zig-zags

Fabricated uniform helices (Rₛ = 1 µm), complex zig-zag (1 µm jumps, 4 min dwells), and stair structures via programmed stage motion.

✍️ Writing Mode

Letters "3D"

Multi-material writing of "3" (Pd) and "D" (Cu) confirmed by EDS mapping. Demonstrates in-situ material switching.

📏 Resolution

< 100 nm

True nanoscale 3D printing. Outperforms conventional AJP (10 µm) by two orders of magnitude. Growth velocity ≈ 15.5 µm/h for helices.

🔗 The Missing Link (Critical GAP)

Paper 3 achieves nanoscale resolution (< 100 nm) and true 3D geometries, but is limited to single metals (Pd, Cu).
Paper 2 demonstrates excellent multi-material capability (polymer + Ag NPs) with perfect dispersion, but is stuck at the microscale (~µm line widths).
Paper 1 highlights that conventional AJP lacks both nano-resolution and simple multi-material mixing.

Research Question

The Next Logical Step

"How can we integrate the electrostatic-guided 3D nanoprinting approach from Paper 3
with the separate-atomization multi-material strategy from Paper 2,
to achieve multi-material, true 3D nanostructures with sub-100 nm resolution in a single hybrid process?"

🎯 Why This Question

  • ✓ P3 proves sub-100 nm 3D structures, but single-metal only.
  • ✓ P2 proves perfect polymer/NP mixing, but micro-scale only.
  • ✓ Combining them enables "Multi-material 3D Nano-printing" – a new paradigm.

🔬 Potential Impact

  • ✓ 3D Nano-electronics: Multi-material interconnects at the nanoscale.
  • ✓ Heterogeneous Sensors: Dielectric + conductor in one 3D nano-architecture.
  • ✓ Meta-materials: Custom optical/mechanical properties with multiple materials.
